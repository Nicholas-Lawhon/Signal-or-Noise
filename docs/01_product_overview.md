# 01 Product Overview — Signal or Noise?

## Working Name

Signal or Noise?

Use the question mark in the working brand because it makes the name feel like a game prompt instead of a generic finance product.

## Taglines

Primary tagline:

> Can you find the signal through the noise?

Secondary tagline:

> Read the clues. Make the call. Beat the reveal.

Product-feature line:

> Play daily, climb leaderboards, and challenge friends.

## One-Sentence Product Description

Signal or Noise? is a mobile-first web game where players are shown disguised historical stock-market scenarios and must decide whether to go long, short, or pass. Players start each challenge with a fictional bankroll, choose their confidence level, and see how much money they would have made or lost after the company and historical outcome are revealed.

## Product Positioning

Signal or Noise? is a market-history guessing game where players test whether they could have spotted winners and losers before the outcome was obvious.

The product should be positioned as a game first, not a finance product.

The intended feel is closer to:

- Wordle
- Trivia games
- Fantasy sports
- Daily challenges
- Competitive guessing games

The intended feel is not:

- Robinhood
- A brokerage app
- A paper-trading terminal
- A stock screener
- A financial advice product
- A finance dashboard

## Core Player Fantasy

The player should feel like they are being dropped into a real historical market moment with incomplete information.

The emotional loop is:

1. Read the scenario.
2. Think: “I might know what this is.”
3. Make a call.
4. Watch the reveal.
5. See whether the signal was obvious in hindsight.
6. Compare the result against friends or the leaderboard.
7. Play another round.

## Primary Audience

The initial audience is casual and competitive players who enjoy:

- Wordle-style daily games
- Trivia
- Fantasy sports
- Prediction games
- Leaderboards
- Short-session mobile games
- Light market/business curiosity

The product should not primarily target hardcore traders. Finance-curious adults may enjoy the game, but the game should not rely on them wanting investing advice.

## Secondary and Future Audiences

Future audiences may include:

- Students
- Kids and families
- Classrooms
- Finance clubs
- Casual investors
- Market-history fans
- Content creators who want shareable challenge results

Education should be treated as a light layer of fun facts, context, and trivia. It should not dominate the game loop.

## Product Pillars

### 1. Game First

Every product decision should prioritize the game loop, clarity, replayability, and fun.

### 2. Mobile First

The web app should be designed for mobile from the beginning. The immediate post-prototype goal is an Expo mobile app that shares logic with the web version.

### 3. Curated Historical Scenarios

The MVP content should use 100 curated scenario cards. These may be AI-assisted, but they must be schema-driven, reviewable, and stored as seed data before being used in the product.

### 4. Fast Decisions, Satisfying Reveals

The player should not feel like they are reading a research report. They should be able to read the card, inspect the lookback chart, choose long/short/pass, select confidence, and quickly reach the reveal.

### 5. Competition Optional, Not Overbearing

Leaderboards, friend challenges, and daily rankings matter, but the product should not feel intimidating or overly serious. The main pitch is a fun guessing game. Competition is a retention layer.

### 6. No Financial Advice

The product should avoid positioning, copy, and features that imply financial advice. Historical market scenarios are for gameplay, trivia, and entertainment.

## MVP Summary

The MVP includes:

- Mobile-first responsive web app
- Single-player Classic Run mode
- Daily Challenge mode
- Hidden-company historical scenario cards
- Long / Short / Pass actions
- Confidence selector
- Fictional bankroll scoring
- Signal Score as a secondary scoring system
- Company reveal
- Score and bankroll breakdown
- Basic user accounts
- Guest play
- Login-gated leaderboard submission
- Basic player profile and stats
- Curated scenario database
- 100 curated scenario cards
- 10 daily challenge pools
- 10 famous market eras
- Admin/content seed system

## Immediate Post-MVP Priorities

After the web prototype works, prioritize:

1. Smart pass scoring.
2. Portfolio Draft mode.
3. Shared package cleanup for mobile readiness.
4. Expo mobile app.
5. Friend challenges and private leagues.
6. Expanded content packs.

## Non-Goals for MVP

The MVP should not include:

- Real-money trading
- Brokerage integrations
- Live market prediction
- Options
- Leverage
- Margin mechanics
- True short-sale mechanics
- Financial advice
- Native mobile app
- Public matchmaking
- Classroom mode
- Dynamic AI-generated live gameplay content
- Crypto/ETF/index scenarios

ETF and crypto support should be documented as future expansion content, not MVP content.

## Product Tone

Default tone:

- Short
- Punchy
- Game-like
- Smart without being academic
- Fun without being goofy
- Competitive without being finance-bro

Example reveal tone:

> That was Netflix. The market was skeptical, but streaming adoption exploded. A bold long call would have crushed this round.

Future tone settings can be added later, but the MVP should ship with one default tone.

## Success Criteria

The prototype is successful if testers:

- Understand the game within one minute.
- Want to play more than one run.
- Enjoy the reveal moment.
- Understand why they won or lost money.
- Compare results with others.
- Ask for more scenarios, daily challenges, or friend modes.
